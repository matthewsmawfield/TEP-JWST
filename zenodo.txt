Temporal Shear: Resolving the High-Redshift Efficiency Crisis

JWST has revealed coherent anomalies at z > 5: star formation efficiencies exceeding ΛCDM limits, overmassive black holes, and stellar masses surpassing dynamical masses — all concentrated in the deepest gravitational potentials. This study tests whether a single violation of the isochrony axiom can account for a systematic fraction of all three. The Temporal Equivalence Principle (TEP), a chameleon-screened scalar-tensor theory, predicts enhanced proper time in unscreened potentials. With a coupling α₀ = 0.58 ± 0.16 calibrated entirely from local Cepheids and zero parameters tuned to JWST data, this single mechanism simultaneously predicts four distinct anomalies: it accounts for ~34% of the Red Monster efficiency excess, resolves all currently identified "impossible" log M* > 10.5 galaxies at z > 8 in this sample (301/400 resolved across z = 7–12), reduces the cosmic SFRD excess from 11.1× to 2.6× ΛCDM, and provides a purely kinematic mechanism (differential temporal shear) for overmassive black hole growth in Little Red Dots.

Four lines of evidence survive mass-control checks across three surveys (N = 4,726): (L1) a dust–Γ_t correlation (ρ = +0.62, N = 1,283) with ΔAIC = −23 against a mass-matched threshold; (L2) inside-out core screening in JADES (ρ = −0.18); (L3) mass–sSFR inversion at z > 7; (L4) resolution of 11/11 impossible M*/M_dyn > 1 cases via SED-independent kinematics, which breaks the mass-proxy circularity. The sharpest model-discriminating result is the Uniformity Paradox: the anomaly is not that massive galaxies at z > 8 are dusty, but that low-mass galaxies are not — in a pattern that tracks gravitational potential depth (ρ = +0.56). Fitted polynomials collapse cross-survey (R² = −6.4) while t_eff generalises without training (ρ = 0.60–0.80). Full CAMB Boltzmann integration is consistent with Planck constraints (σ₈ within 0.1σ). The clustering-corrected combined significance is 6.4σ (upper bound).

Eight new cross-dataset results further strengthen the evidence. (1) COSMOS2025 dust (Shuntov et al. 2025; 0.54 deg² blank field): partial ρ(Γ_t, E(B−V) | M*, z) strengthens from +0.241 at z = 4–7 to +0.595 at z = 9–13 (N = 1,568, 95% CI [+0.532, +0.660]) — noting that the UNCOVER DR4 Prospector spec-z subsample (N = 122) returns a null (ρ = −0.009, p = 0.92) at z = 9–12, plausibly explained by NIRSpec incompleteness against dusty systems at z > 9. (2) COSMOS2025 sSFR inversion: partial ρ flips from −0.010 (null) at z = 4–7 to +0.237 at z = 9–13; Steiger Z = 6.37 (p = 1.9 × 10⁻¹⁰) supports L3 in an independent field. (3) UNCOVER DR4 MegaScience (Wang et al. 2024): null at z < 7, then ρ = +0.514 at z = 8–9. (4) DJA GOODS-S morphology (Yang et al. 2025; 7,325 spec-confirmed): A_V partial ρ = +0.243 replicates L1 in a fourth field. (5) DJA NIRSpec Balmer decrement (de Graaff et al. 2024; N = 2,925): spectroscopic Hα/Hβ dust measurement (no SED fitting) yields partial ρ = +0.243 (p = 1.6 × 10⁻⁴⁰, 95% CI [+0.205, +0.280]) — the first spectroscopic support for L1. (6) TEP mass measurement bias: explicitly debiasing mass measurements strengthens partial correlations by ~1.5×–1.9×. (7) Multi-dataset Fisher combination gives z = 30.0σ across 5 independent fields/pipelines. (8) L1 and L3 are mathematically orthogonal: controlling for sSFR leaves the z > 9 dust partial correlation unchanged at +0.592. Key limitations: (i) Γ_t is a deterministic function of photometric halo mass, so breaking the mass-proxy degeneracy definitively requires IFU velocity dispersions; (ii) the dust–Γ_t correlation (L1) contributes ~90% of the combined statistical weight.
